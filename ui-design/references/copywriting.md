# Copywriting Reference
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6 (Copywriting), complete chapter

---

## Be concise
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Every element of your interface should earn its place, especially the words. More words do not result in more communication, as people often will not read large chunks of text.

If a word can be removed without losing information or negatively affecting usability, remove it. Being concise makes content faster and easier to understand.

Guidelines on how to be concise:
- Avoid unneeded words like: "actually," "basically," "really," "truthfully," and "quite."
- Avoid short joining words like: "a," "an," and "the."
- Avoid introductory phrases like: "would you like to," "in order to," "when it comes to," "are you sure," "there are," and "it is."
- Use shorter words instead of longer ones if they convey the same message.
- Try to keep sentences under 20 words. People can lose focus when reading long sentences with a lot of commas.

Example from the book: "Would you like to save the article? Don't worry, you'll still be able to publish it at a later date. You can always find saved articles in your library." becomes "Save article?" with a subtitle "Save the article to your library to publish later."

## Use sentence case
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Sentence case: only the first word and proper nouns are capitalised. Example: "This is sentence case."

Title case capitalises all words except minor words. Title case is harder to read: capital letters interrupt scanning and confuse our expectation of a lower case letter. This unnecessarily increases cognitive load.

---

## Use plain and simple language
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Many people have trouble reading and some have mental impairments. Keep language simple and conversational to make it easier for a broader range of people to understand.

Tips:
- Imagine having a conversation with a 6th grade student who is unfamiliar with the topic.
- Avoid jargon (specialised or technical language understood only by particular groups).
- Avoid slang (very informal language used between people in the same social group).
- Choose short, simple words over long and complex ones.
- Use shortened words to sound more natural: who's, they're, you're.

## Front-load text
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Front-loading is putting the important information at the start of your text. People quickly scan text down the page from left to right. Putting the key information or benefit at the front of headings, lists, and links makes it faster and easier for people to get value.

Examples without front-loading (key information bolded for clarity):
- "Subscribe to my newsletter to **learn UI design**"
- "Sign up today for **30% off**"
- "You should read these **5 UI design eBooks**."

Rewritten with front-loading:
- "**Learn UI design** by subscribing to my newsletter"
- "**30% off** if you sign up today"
- "**5 UI design eBooks** you should read."

## Use the inverted pyramid
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

The inverted pyramid is a writing structure where the most important information is placed at the start, followed by supporting information and smaller background details.

Why it works for interface text:
- Gets to the point quickly, helping people understand information and make decisions faster.
- People who quickly skim the first sentence still get the main point.
- Additional information is there for those who need it.

How to apply it:
- Most important information in the heading, allowing most people to complete the task without reading anything else.
- Supporting information displayed under the heading for those who need more detail.
- Smaller background details moved to a separate screen, as they are less important and could add unnecessary complexity.

## Limit the use of abbreviations and acronyms
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Abbreviations save space but make people think, which increases cognitive load.

If you do use them, explain what they mean initially. Example: "The ETA (estimated time of arrival) of the dept. manager is COB (close of business) tomorrow."

Better: remove all abbreviations. "The estimated time of arrival of the department manager is close of business tomorrow." Longer but clearer.

Form field example: "Apt. no." becomes "Apartment number."

## Limit the use of UPPERCASE
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Unless shouting at people, there are not many valid reasons to use uppercase. Uppercase words all have the same rectangular shape, forcing you to read each letter one by one rather than recognising the word shape.

That being said, uppercase can be used for short labels to visually differentiate them from other text. Use uppercase in bold weight at a small size and increase the letter spacing. This makes it less loud and more legible.

Example from the book: 18px regular weight 0px letter spacing category label versus 14px bold weight 2px letter spacing category label.

## Break up content using descriptive headings and bullets
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Break up large pieces of information into multiple smaller ones. Highlight key information using descriptive headings. This allows people to quickly scan and understand the structure and organisation of content.

Make sure headings are descriptive for two reasons:
1. Many people quickly scan information and may not read supporting text.
2. Screen reader users often listen to a list of all headings on a page and quickly skip to the information they are after. Headings must make sense out of context.

Example: "Property features" with three vague subheadings ("Location," "Check-in," "Parking") becomes three descriptive subheadings: "Beautiful waterfront location," "Fast check-in experience," "Free secure parking."

## Avoid using "my" on form labels
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Think of the interface as a person talking to the user. A field labelled "my email address" refers to the interface's email address, not the user's.

Using "your" is clearer but unnecessary in most cases. Avoid both "my" and "your" if possible. Be concise.

Example: "My email" or "Your email" both simplify to just "Email."

---

## Use vocabulary consistently
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Use the same word consistently to describe the same interface element.

Example: if the primary button on a product page is "Add to cart" but the cart is labelled "Bag," this causes confusion.

Common examples of inconsistent vocabulary:
- Publish vs. post
- Sign up vs. register
- Log in vs. sign in
- Delete vs. remove
- Subscribe vs. join

---

## Use numerals for numbers
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Use numerals rather than spelling them out as text. Numerals are easier to scan (they have a different shape to letters), quicker to read and understand, and much more concise.

For large numbers, use a combination of numerals and letters. Example: "1 billion" not "1,000,000,000."

Format numerals consistently and use commas to break them up. Example: 1,000, not 1000.

---

## Avoid full stops if possible
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Most interface text is short enough that full stops (periods) are not needed. Do not add full stops unless the text forms a full sentence with commas.

Removing unnecessary full stops simplifies the interface. If you have multiple similar interface elements next to each other, be consistent about whether you use full stops across all of them.

---

## Ensure text length is similar across similar interface elements
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

If you have multiple blocks of text aligned in a row, try to ensure they are the same length. The more interface elements aligned with each other, the more organised and simple the interface looks.

---

## Ensure text links describe their destination
**Source**: Practical UI, Chapter 6

Text links must describe where they will take the user when actioned. Using generic text like "learn more," "read more," or "click here" is problematic:
- Harder to scan: you are forced to read surrounding text to gain context.
- Screen reader users often listen to a list of all text links on a page. Generic links do not make sense when read out of context.
- "Click here" is unnecessary (most people already know links can be actioned) and inaccurate for those not using a mouse.

Fix: use the name of the destination page. Example: instead of three identical "Learn more" links, use descriptive links: "Explore templates," "Email marketing features," "How affiliates work."

Another option: remove the text link and turn the heading itself into a link.
