# The 5 Rings of Buying Insight — full reference

Source method: Jim Kraus & Adele Revella, *Buyer Personas: Revised and Expanded* (Buyer Persona Institute). This file is the working reference for the `buyer-personas` skill. Read it before extracting insights.

The whole point: buyers don't just choose between you and a competitor — many choose to **do nothing** (status quo) out of fear of making a bad decision. A persona must give marketing/sales what they need across three situations: (1) helping buyers choose the best option, (2) overcoming indecision, and (3) beating the status quo. The 5 Rings deliver exactly that.

---

## Ring 1 — Priority Initiatives (PI): what triggers the search

The **most compelling reason** buyers decide to invest in a solution like yours *now* — and why others stay content with the status quo. It describes the personal/organizational circumstances that pushed this to the top of the list and made them willing to spend time, budget, or political capital.

- It is the **triggering moment** — when the pain of living with the problem (or the upside of solving it) finally got attention. Not "we needed better X" but *what changed* to make it urgent now.
- Tells you **when buyers are receptive** and **who** sets the decision in motion.

**Extraction prompt:** "Using only the provided sources: What were the immediate needs or events that triggered the decision to look for [solution] *now*? What changed to make it a priority rather than something they'd lived with? Annotate the source of each."

---

## Ring 2 — Success Factors (SF): the outcomes they expect

The operational or **personal results** buyers expect from the purchase. Resembles benefits — but stated as *the buyer's* expected outcomes, not your guessed-at value props. May be business outcomes (reduce risk, grow revenue, fewer callbacks) or personal/aspirational (look good to the boss, control something, peace of mind).

**Extraction prompt:** "Using only the provided sources: What outcomes did buyers need to achieve with [solution]? Include performance, operational, financial, and personal results. Annotate the source of each."

---

## Ring 3 — Perceived Barriers (PB): the "bad news" ring

What **prevents** buyers from considering you, or why they believe a competitor is better — and the **fears** that cause indecision or keep them with the status quo. Often: people who got in the way, prior bad experiences, missing capabilities, price concerns, risk of getting it wrong.

This is the most actionable "bad news." Knowing the barriers tells you what to reassure.

**Extraction prompt:** "Using only the provided sources: What concerns, doubts, or fears did buyers have about making this investment, about choosing us, or that pushed them toward a competitor or toward doing nothing? Annotate the source of each."

---

## Ring 4 — Decision Criteria (DC): what they compare

The specific **attributes / capabilities / aspects** of the product, service, company, or experience that buyers evaluate as they compare alternatives. The questions they ask to winnow options. Often surprises marketers — newest/most-distinctive features may matter least; facts may beat benefits-speak; service attributes ("treat us like we matter") count.

**Extraction prompt:** "Using only the provided sources: What did buyers evaluate and compare across options? What questions did they have about the solution's capabilities and about the companies themselves? Annotate the source of each."

### SF vs DC — the disambiguation that trips people up
- **Success Factors = WHY** (the outcome the buyer wants — "so we can serve more patients / stop getting callbacks").
- **Decision Criteria = WHAT / HOW** (the capability they compare to get that outcome — "scan time," "same-day service," "integrated coil").
- DC are the attributes buyers believe they need *in order to achieve* their SF outcomes.
- A single quote can contain both: the capability (DC) and the reason it matters (SF). Split it across both rings.

---

## Ring 5 — Buyer's Journey (BJ): who, steps, sources

The behind-the-scenes story: which **influencers/roles** are involved at each phase, what each did, how much sway they had, the **steps** taken to evaluate and eliminate options, and the **information sources** they trusted (websites, peers, reviews, demos, site visits, conferences) — *with the reason each was trusted and what they learned there*.

Used to align sales/marketing to the right people, at the right step, with the right resource.

**Extraction prompt:** "Using only the provided sources: Who was involved in the decision and what role did each play? What steps did buyers take to find and narrow options? What information sources did they use and trust, and what did they learn from each? Annotate the source of each."

> Note: top themes are drawn from Rings 1–4 only; the Buyer's Journey informs *channel/sequence*, not the core message themes.

## Source selection — the four buyer groups

When choosing whose words to mine (and what to ask the user to gather), rank by insight value:

1. **Chose a competitor** — most valuable. They articulate exactly where you fell short and are often *eager* to tell the story. Hardest to obtain (sales rarely surfaces lost deals).
2. **Chose the status quo** (started looking, did nothing) — reveals indecision/fear; only covers early stages.
3. **Never considered you, chose a competitor** — reveals why you weren't even in the running; hardest to find.
4. **Chose you** — easiest to get, but only shows where you got it right. Useful for reinforcing what's working; **do not over-weight.**

Do **not** interview/transcribe people *currently* in an open deal — wait until their decision is complete.

You need **fewer sources than you'd think** — patterns repeat fast. ~10 solid buyer accounts is a good checkpoint; you'll often hear near-identical stories well before that. Note when a Ring is supported by only one buyer (weak) vs. several (strong).

## Mining mechanics (the "Insights Aggregator")

Conceptually a table per Ring with four columns:

| Buyer Quote (verbatim) | Source | Main Point (shorthand) | Buying Insight Headline (buyer's voice) |
|---|---|---|---|

1. Paste verbatim quotes under the right Ring, with source.
2. Write a shorthand **Main Point** for each; adjust wording so similar quotes cluster; sort to group them.
3. Write the **Buying Insight Headline** — phrased as the buyer directly asking/stating it, detailed enough that a stakeholder who reads *only* the headlines still gets it. (e.g. DC headline: *"How sharp, clear, and detailed is the imaging quality?"*)
4. **One quote per buyer per insight.** If an insight has 4+ quotes from different buyers, consider splitting it into two sharper insights. If only one quote, consider folding it into a related insight — but never force a connection; leave true outliers aside until more data supports them.

---

## Top themes (final synthesis)

After the rings are built, step back and find **5–8 higher-order needs** by looking *across* Rings 1–4. Each theme usually links several insights. Tag each theme with the contributing rings (PI/SF/PB/DC). Litmus test: read the themes back — if they capture the primary expectations you heard, you're done; if something nags as missing, find the gap. These themes are what messaging, web copy, and sales enablement should lead with.

---

## Faithfulness rules (the gate — apply before delivering)

1. **Evidence or nothing.** Every insight, theme, and profile field traces to actual buyer-derived input. No input → no persona; stop and ask for sources. Never fabricate to fill a ring.
2. **Verbatim quotes, lightly edited only.** Remove filler, repeated words, half-formed phrases. **Never** insert your own words, sanitize, summarize the point away, or strip colorful phrasing. A grammatically ugly but authentic quote beats a clean invented one.
3. **Annotate every source.** Each quote shows where it came from (call/buyer/date). This lets the user cross-check.
4. **Flag gaps honestly.** If a Ring is thin or empty, or if all sources are happy customers (Group 1), say so explicitly in the persona header. Don't paper over it.
5. **No demographic theater.** Skip age/gender/ethnicity/photo unless a characteristic genuinely changes a marketing decision. If naming the persona, prefer gender-neutral and skip the photo to avoid stakeholder debates about irrelevant traits.
6. **One decision, one mindset.** If the evidence splits into two distinct buyer mindsets, build two personas rather than averaging them into mush.
7. **AI is a first pass, not the final word.** Using a model to mine transcripts speeds up an *initial* set of insights; it doesn't replace human judgment on nuance, headline wording, and best-quote selection. Present results as a draft for the user to refine, and note low-confidence calls.

## If gathering raw material yourself (interview technique, condensed)

When the user needs to *run* buyer interviews to create the inputs:

- **Open with one scripted question:** *"Take me back to the day you first realized you needed [category of solution] — tell me what happened."* Start at the trigger moment, before they ever heard of you. No small talk.
- **Probe with the buyer's own words.** Jot keywords; return with "Tell me more about… / What was important about…". Buyers first answer with benefits — push past that to the *why now*.
- **Go slowly, chronologically.** Don't jump to the end. Park asterisks for threads to revisit.
- **Don't structure the interview around the 5 Rings.** That keeps focus on you. Let them tell the story; the rings are for *organizing findings afterward*.
- **"How did you decide…"** beats "why did you decide" (less confrontational, invites detail) — especially for how they eliminated an option.
- **Probe jargon** ("easy to use," "scalable," "responsive") — that's where specific decision criteria hide.
- **Ask who else was involved** (roles, not names) to map the buyer's journey.
- For a lost deal / missing feature / "too expensive": ask open-endedly what the pricier/other option had, then test a prepared value statement: *"We hear buyers value [advantage] because [outcome] — what's your take?"* — never slide into a sales pitch.
- Save any profile/demographic questions for the **end**.
