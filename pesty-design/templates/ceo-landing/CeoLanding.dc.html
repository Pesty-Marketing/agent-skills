Our sites work. The way they're built is what limits us.

When we had a handful of clients, the way our sites were wired together was perfectly fine. But the business has grown. Some clients now have 200+ service areas — and those pages already generate automatically. What hasn't kept up is the structure underneath them: how each area connects to its state, county, city and ZIP codes. At that scale, the missing structure becomes the bottleneck.

This isn't about fixing something broken. It's about replacing a foundation that was right for 10 sites with one designed for 100.

The one idea to hold onto

It comes down to one question: are we writing data down, or connecting it?

Imagine saving every client's phone number into a single note, separated by commas. You can read them — but you can't tap one to call, can't sort them, can't search “who's in this area.” For that, each number has to be a real contact the phone understands. That's the difference between how our sites store information today and how they will.

What's slowing us down today

Four things that don't survive 200+ locations.

01

The pages generate themselves — the homepage around them doesn't

The service-area pages themselves already generate automatically from our data. What doesn't is everything that should be derived from those areas — the “Locations We Service” pills, ZIP-code lists and related-location links on the homepage. None of them are connected to the areas, so each is maintained by hand. Add a new area and its page appears, but it won't show up in those lists until someone adds it there too.

What it costs us Every new area generates its page — but the homepage lists, ZIP counts and links around it still get updated by hand. That's the work that doesn't survive 200+ locations.
02

Information we have, but the system can't use

ZIP codes are stored as one long line of text. To a person it reads fine. To the system it's a single blob — so it can't connect a ZIP to its city or county, can't group them, can't build pages from them.

What it costs us We have the data but can't put it to work. Anything dynamic has to be rebuilt by hand instead.
03

No consistent rule for where things belong

There's no shared rule for what goes where. On one client site, locations are filed under “pests.” On another, pests are filed under “service areas.” Each site is organized differently.

“

It's like a filing cabinet where one office keeps towns in the “Customers” drawer and another keeps suppliers there. No new hire — and no automation — can reliably pull a clean list.

What it costs us Nothing is portable. We can't reuse, automate, or standardize across clients, because every site is arranged its own way.
04

Locations aren't connected to each other

Today a service area isn't linked to its city, county, or state in any structured way, and “nearby” areas are set one by one. So nothing can flow automatically: an area can't inherit its own ZIP codes or nearby locations, and the homepage can't pull from real records. Everything is duplicated by hand, which invites inconsistencies.

Reassurance

What already works, we keep.

Our SEO landing pages stay.

Dedicated pages like “Ant Control in Ashland, VA” rank better than a generic page — a smart, deliberate strategy. We keep it, and they already generate automatically. What improves is that each one can finally inherit its own ZIP codes and nearby areas from connected data, instead of us maintaining that separately. Same pages, richer and self-updating.

Curation stays.

We still choose which locations to feature on the homepage — often grouped by county. That editorial control doesn't go away. The list simply feeds itself from real location records, with a control to pick which ones to highlight. We keep the choice, we drop the hand-building.

The new foundation

One template, built on connected data.

The pages already generate. What we're adding underneath is a connected geographic model — every service area explicitly linked to its state, county, city and ZIP codes. With one source of truth, everything that should derive from a location finally can — and a new client goes live mainly by swapping in their data.
