# Audit Checklist

For each failure: state what is broken, quote the offending copy, suggest the exact fix.

---

## Opening check

- [ ] Does the first sentence name change, or the threat of change?
- [ ] Is there a curiosity gap in the first paragraph — something the reader thinks they have some idea about but isn't sure?
- [ ] Does the opening avoid throat-clearing ("In today's world…", "It's no secret that…", "Founded in 20XX…")?

## Character check

- [ ] Is there ONE named protagonist (founder, customer, reader-as-hero)?
- [ ] Is the protagonist's **sacred flaw** identifiable from the copy — a flawed theory of control they have made identity-defining?
- [ ] Is the flaw shown through behaviour, not announced through adjectives?
- [ ] Is the protagonist described in their **imperfections**, not their virtues? (A character's value is in their flaws.)

## Theory of control check

- [ ] Does the copy show the flaw *working* before it shows it breaking? (The hero-maker moment must be there or the reader has no investment.)
- [ ] Is the **story event** — the thing that overwhelms the flaw — concrete and specific to *this* character, not a generic shock?
- [ ] Does the ignition point strike at the *specific* flaw, not the protagonist generally?

## Arc check

- [ ] Does the piece move through the 5-act spine: *This is me, it's not working → Is there another way? → I have transformed → Can I handle the pain? → Who am I going to be?*
- [ ] Is **Act IV (the pain of change)** present? Skipping it makes the transformation unearned.
- [ ] Does the ending deliver a **God moment** — a specific, visual instant of restored control over both external world and internal self?

## Dramatic question check

- [ ] Is the question *"Who is this person?"* implicitly posed and answered by the end?
- [ ] Does the protagonist make a final choice that reveals who they have become?

## Craft check

- [ ] Does the copy use **filmic word order** (subject → verb → object) and active voice?
- [ ] Are descriptions specific (three sensory qualities per object that matters) — not adjectival blur?
- [ ] Are metaphors **bodily** (rough, heavy, sharp, cold) — not abstract or worn-out?
- [ ] Is there a **"because"** between scenes, not just "and then"?
- [ ] Is cause and effect *shown*, not announced?

## Environment check

- [ ] When physical places appear, do they describe character through identity claims / feeling regulators / behavioural residue — not just décor?
- [ ] Are settings named specifically (the actual city, the actual room, the actual year) — not generic ("an office," "a conference")?

## Brand-as-hero check

- [ ] Does the copy avoid making the brand or product the hero?
- [ ] Is the brand present as the **agent of change** — what lets the protagonist rebuild their theory of control — rather than as the savior?
- [ ] Is the customer (or founder) still the one making the decisive choice?

## Pacing check

- [ ] Are the most important moments (ignition, Act III reversal, God moment) **slowed down** with sensory detail?
- [ ] Are bridging passages compressed?
- [ ] Does every paragraph either advance the arc or close an objection? (If not, cut it.)

## Voice check

- [ ] If first-person, does the voice match the worksheet's Step 5 calibration?
- [ ] Are there specific phrases, rhythms, diction that this character would actually use — or has it dissolved into "professional brand voice"?

---

## Key Rules (Never Violate)

- **Change opens every story.** Steady-state openings forfeit the brain's attention.
- **The character's value is in their imperfections.** Goodness is infertile terrain.
- **Pin the origin damage to a specific scene.** You may cut the scene; you must know it.
- **Customer = hero. Brand = agent.** Brand-as-hero kills the arc.
- **The story event must strike the specific flaw.** A generic shock is not a story.
- **Show theory of control breaking — don't announce it.**
- **Act IV is mandatory.** Skipping the pain of change makes the transformation unearned.
- **The God moment is concrete.** A specific image, not a generic "they're better now."
- **All description is character description.** No neutral detail exists.
- **"Because," not "and then."** Every scene transition is causal or the scene is cut.
- **Specificity is everything.** Names, sensory detail, body-metaphors. Abstraction loses the reader.

---

## Audit on very short copy (taglines, ads)

Not every Storr element fits in 10 words. For micro-copy, scope the audit to:

1. **Change** — does the line carry the energy of something shifting?
2. **Specificity** — concrete, sensory, named — or abstract?
3. **Voice** — does it sound like a person, or like a brand?

Everything else moves up to the page that surrounds the micro-copy.

---

## Audit on long-form non-fiction (essays, longform sales)

Apply the full checklist plus:

- [ ] Is the dramatic question front-loaded? (Can the reader articulate the central question after the first 200 words?)
- [ ] Does the piece earn its length — every section either advances the arc or closes an objection?
- [ ] Is the ending a *resolution*, not a summary? (Storr: brains love control restored. Summaries deny that.)
