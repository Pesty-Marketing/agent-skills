# Craft Rules — Line-Level Engineering

Storr's brain-level rules for keeping a reader inside the model. Apply at draft time, not at outline time.

---

## Openings

**Change is what the brain is alert to.** Open with change, or its threat, or its trace. Never with steady state.

- *"Where's Papa going with that ax?"* — Charlotte's Web
- *"Mr and Mrs Dursley… were proud to say they were perfectly normal, thank you very much."* — Harry Potter (pregnant with threat)
- *"The night the third customer in a month asked for a refund, he sat in the truck and stopped pretending."*

**The curiosity gap is shaped like a lowercase *n*.** Curiosity peaks when the reader thinks they have *some* idea but isn't sure. Four ways to trigger it:

1. Pose a puzzle
2. Set up a sequence with an anticipated but unknown resolution
3. Violate an expectation
4. Plant evidence that *someone else* knows

If you give the answer in the first sentence, the curve flattens and the reader leaves.

---

## Sentence Mechanics

**Filmic word order.** The brain models the sentence in real time as it reads. Order the words like a camera move.

> Worse: *"Jane gave her Dad a kitten."*
> Better: *"Jane gave a kitten to her Dad."*

The image lands cleaner because the camera follows: Jane → action → kitten → recipient.

**Active voice.** Passive voice removes the actor, which mutes the change. Use passive only when the actor is genuinely unknown or unimportant.

---

## Showing, Not Telling

**Three specific qualities per object that matters.**

> Lewis: *"Instead of telling us a thing was 'terrible,' describe it so we'll be terrified."*

> Süskind, *Perfume* — opens by stacking eighteenth-century stenches in one paragraph until the reader is choking on the air of the world.

In business writing, this looks like:
- Not *"the kitchen was messy"*
- But *"three days of mail on the island, the dog's bowl licked clean and skidded into the cabinet, a single mug in the sink with a coffee ring like a halo"*

**Naming = world-building.** Specific names ("the Kessel Run," "Tannhäuser Gate") give the brain something to anchor on. Generic nouns dissolve. Name the conference room, the city, the product variant, the year.

---

## Metaphor

**Every metaphor should activate a body sense.** "Rough day" lights up touch regions in the brain; "bad day" doesn't.

- *Heavy* news
- A *sharp* email
- A *cold* market
- A *brittle* assumption

**Avoid worn-out metaphors.** They no longer fire neurons. *"At the end of the day," "moving the needle," "low-hanging fruit," "rocked the boat"* — all dead. Replace with one that still has body in it.

---

## Cause and Effect

**Between every scene, you should be able to say "because" — never "and then."**

> McKenna: *"You want all your scenes to have a 'because' between them, and not an 'and then'."*

If a scene exists only because it happened next in the timeline, cut it or rewrite it so it earns its place causally.

**But don't over-explain.** Cause and effect should be *shown*, not announced. The reader's brain wants the gap — that's where their model gets to assemble itself.

> Bad: *"Because he was tired from the long day, he was short with the customer."*
> Better: *"He counted the receipts twice. Then a third time. When the customer asked for the manager he said 'You're talking to him' without looking up."*

---

## Environment Describes Character

Three categories of environmental detail (Sam Gosling's research):

- **Identity claims**: things meant to broadcast who they are — certificates, books, posters, the framed first-dollar.
- **Feeling regulators**: things meant to manage their own emotional state — candles, family photos turned toward them, comfort objects.
- **Behavioural residue**: traces of what they actually do when no one's watching — the dent in the wall, the takeout containers, the stack of unread industry magazines.

When describing a founder's office, a customer's home, a competitor's storefront — choose details across these three categories and the reader builds the character without you saying anything direct about them.

---

## Theory of Mind Layering

**The richest scenes ask: what does A think B is thinking about A?**

> Mackendrick's drill. Two characters in a room. Each is reading the other reading them. Every line either confirms or destabilises that reading.

In business storytelling: customer thinks the brand thinks they're a small account. Brand thinks the customer thinks they're getting fleeced. The conversation unfolds in the gaps.

---

## Pacing

**Detail stretches time. Bare summary collapses it.**

When you want a moment to land — the ignition point, the God moment, the worst beat of Act IV — *slow down*. Sensory detail. Theory of mind. A single line of dialogue. Then resume normal pace.

If you describe everything at the same speed, nothing feels important.

---

## What to Cut

Storr's quiet rule: a scene that doesn't either (a) advance the plot or (b) stand on its own as drama is dead weight. Cut it.

Same applies to a paragraph in non-fiction. If a paragraph doesn't either (a) advance the arc or (b) close an objection, cut it.

---

## Voice

**The character's voice is built in Step 5 of the worksheet** (the Baldwin exercise). Once you've written 300 words in their voice, every later sentence in their POV should match that pitch.

For founder narratives: write the founder's voice, not "professional brand voice." The juice is in specificity of speech. Their actual phrases. Their actual rhythms. Their actual diction.

---

## A few Storr lines to keep in earshot

- *"Change is endlessly fascinating to brains."*
- *"Who we are is how we're broken."*
- *"All description works as a description of character."*
- *"Brains love control. It's their heaven."*
- *"All story is gossip."*
- *"Goodness is infertile terrain for a writer."*

These are diagnostic. If your draft contradicts any of them, the draft is the problem.
