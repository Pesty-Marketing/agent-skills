# Audit Checklist

For each failure: state what is broken, quote the offending copy, suggest the exact fix.

---

## Empathy and Reader Focus

- [ ] **Customer-centric**: Is the reader in the first paragraph? Or does it start with company history/mission/awards?
- [ ] **It's-me Minute**: Would the target reader feel "this describes me exactly"? Or is the writing generic enough to apply to anyone?
- [ ] **WIIFM clear**: Is the benefit to the reader explicit — not implied, not buried in paragraph 3?
- [ ] **You > We**: Does the copy use "you/your" more than "we/our/I"? Company-centric writing is the default failure mode.

## Structure and Openings

- [ ] **No running start**: Does the piece start with the actual point? Or does it warm up with background, context-setting, or "In today's world…" throat-clearing?
- [ ] **Lead hooks**: Does the opening give the reader a reason to keep reading (story, stat, question, bold claim)?
- [ ] **Kicker lands**: Does the ending wrap the narrative with a clear next step or emotional resonance? Or does it just stop?
- [ ] **Most important words first**: Do sentences and paragraphs lead with their strongest point — or bury it at the end?

## Voice and Word Choice

- [ ] **No Frankenwords**: No unnecessarily bulked-up words (-ization, -ation, -ment when a simpler form exists)?
- [ ] **No Weblish**: No tech-robot words applied to humans (bandwidth, ping, leverage, learnings)?
- [ ] **No buzzwords**: No synergy, cutting-edge, revolutionary, world-class, seamless, impactful, game-changing?
- [ ] **Contractions used**: Does the copy use contractions naturally, or does it sound like a legal document?
- [ ] **Active voice**: Are sentences in active voice? Or is the passive voice hiding the subject?
- [ ] **Strong verbs**: Are verbs carrying the sentences, or are weakling be-verbs doing all the work?
- [ ] **Adverbs minimal**: Are -ly adverbs present that could be cut or replaced with a stronger verb?

## Show vs. Tell

- [ ] **Evidence over adjectives**: Are claims backed by specifics (numbers, named examples, concrete details)? Or does the copy rely on adjectives ("world-class," "dedicated," "experienced")?
- [ ] **Sensory/concrete**: Can the reader picture what success looks like? Or is the outcome described in abstract terms?

## Scannability

- [ ] **Passes the scan test**: Does the structure (headers, bold, bullets) hold up if a reader skims without reading every word?
- [ ] **No wall of text**: Are paragraphs short enough to invite reading, not intimidate?

## Voice Consistency (if brand voice exists)

- [ ] **Voice adjectives present**: Does the copy reflect the brand's defined voice words?
- [ ] **Tone appropriate to context**: Is the tone calibrated to the situation? (Humor appropriate; not deployed during complaint resolution.)
- [ ] **Logo test**: If you masked the logo, would this copy sound like the brand — or like anyone?

---

## Common Failure Patterns

**"About Us" pages**: Usually the worst offender — all company, no customer. Almost always starts with founding story. Fix: open with the customer's problem.

**Homepage hero sections**: Often generic ("The #1 platform for X"). Fix: make the customer the subject, name their specific problem.

**Email subject lines**: Often vague teasers or pure announcements. Fix: make the benefit to the reader explicit in the subject line itself.

**Blog introductions**: Almost always have a running start ("Content marketing is more important than ever…"). Fix: cut the first paragraph and check if meaning survives.

**Product descriptions**: Often list features. Fix: translate every feature into a customer outcome using "so you can..." framing.
