# Writing Rules

## The Foundational Mindset

**Pathological Empathy**: Write from the reader's perspective, not the company's. The reader pays with their attention. Every sentence must earn it.

**WIIFM (What's In It For Me)**: Before writing anything, identify the specific benefit to the reader. If you can't name it, don't write yet.

**It's-me Minute**: Does your reader feel seen, recognized, understood? The best copy makes readers think "Wow, this describes me exactly."

- Company-centric: "Outsource your long to-do list to a Virtual Assistant."
- Customer-centric: "You spend evenings and weekends answering emails instead of enjoying your free time. You're here because something needs to change."

**H2H (Human to Human)**: Write as if you are a person talking to another person across a kitchen table. Not a brand talking at a market segment.

## The Writing GPS

Three phases. Execute in order.

### GO (prep)
1. **Goal** — What business outcome does this content serve? If you don't care about what you're writing, no one will.
2. **So What? / Because** — Reframe goal to reader benefit. Ask "So what? Because..." until exhausted. Last answer = real angle.
3. **Add data** — Find credible evidence to support your point. Data comes alive when paired with human stories.
4. **Organize** — Choose a structure before drafting. (See: 15 structures below)

### PUSH (draft)
5. **Ugly First Draft (TUFD)** — Show up and throw up. Write badly on purpose. No editing. Just get it out.
6. **Walk away** — Brain break. Return with fresh eyes.
7. **Draft 2: Cross out the wrong words** — Cut anything that doesn't advance the story. Trim the running start.
8. **Draft 3: Swap places with your reader** — Read as if you've never heard of the brand. Does it serve the reader or the company? Replace every "we/I/our" with "you/your" wherever possible.
9. **Add voice** — This is where personality enters. See voice-grammar.md.
10. **Headline** — Write last. The headline is the promise; write it after you know what you delivered.

### SHINE (polish)
11. **Robot edit** — Spellcheck, grammar tools, readability score.
12. **Read it aloud** — If you stumble reading it, readers will too. Fix every stumble.
13. **Eyeball for scanners** — Most readers scan before they read. Does the structure hold up with only headers and bolded text?
14. **Let it go with love** — Ship. Done is better than perfect.

## Leads and Kickers

**The Lead**: Hook the reader immediately. Do not start with context, history, or "In today's fast-paced world." Options:
- A story or scene
- A startling statistic
- A direct question
- A bold claim

**The Kicker**: The ending does not fade out. It wraps the narrative and gives the reader a clear next step or final emotional resonance. Never just stop.

**The Running Start**: Common mistake — starting with too much background and setup before getting to the actual point.

> Before (running start): "Simultaneous to the modern boom of Web 2.0 and along with the rise of social media, companies have projected their presence..."
> After: "Many companies succeed via Facebook and Twitter. But only a fraction have explored YouTube marketing."

Rule: Try cutting your first paragraph entirely. If the piece still makes sense, cut it.

## Show, Don't Tell

Replace vague adjectives with concrete evidence and sensory detail.

| Instead of | Write |
|---|---|
| "Our customer service is world-class." | "Our support team answers every call within three rings, 24/7." |
| "A better way to learn to cook." | "Learn to cook without recipes in 30 days." |
| "We're experienced." | "We've served 14,000 homeowners since 2008." |
| "Our team is passionate." | "Our technicians are licensed in 3 states and re-train every quarter." |

Rule: Any sentence with an adjective — ask: "Can I prove this?" If yes, replace the adjective with the proof. If no, cut it.

---

## Place the Most Important Words First

Start sentences and paragraphs with the most critical information. English buries meaning at the end.

> Bad: "In order to help you save time, we created this app."
> Better: "Save time with this app."

> Bad: "We have noticed that four-day workweeks have become standard."
> Better: "Four-day workweeks are now standard."

## 15 Ways to Organize (GPS Structure Options)

1. How-to
2. List (numbered or bulleted)
3. Problem/solution
4. Before/after
5. Question/answer
6. News/announcement
7. Comparison
8. Personal story
9. Case study
10. Interview/Q&A
11. Definition/explainer
12. Analogy or metaphor
13. Data-driven argument
14. Counter-intuitive take
15. Narrative/story arc

## The Dear Mom Technique

If a draft sounds stiff, rewrite the opening as if explaining the concept to your mother or a friend who loves you. Write "Dear Mom," then write naturally. Then delete "Dear Mom" and keep the rest.

Purpose: strips corporate marketing-speak, uncoils the voice into something natural and friendly.

---

## Approach Writing Like Teaching

Your job is not to show off what you know. Your job is to transfer what you know into the reader's head as clearly and efficiently as possible.

- What does the reader need to know?
- What's the single most important thing to leave them with?
- What would confuse them? Cut it.

---

## Content as Gift

The best content makes readers feel seen and helps them. It solves problems, shoulders burdens, eases pain, enriches their lives.

Content written only to serve SEO or company goals is a wasted opportunity. Content created for the reader compounds: people share it, return to it, cite it.
