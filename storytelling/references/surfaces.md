# Output Surfaces

Applications of the Sacred Flaw worksheet to specific narrative surfaces. Each surface assumes Steps 1-8 of the worksheet are already filled in.

---

## Founder Narrative / About Page

The most natural fit for Storr's framework. The founder is the hero. The flaw is what made the company necessary.

### Structure (5-section template)

1. **Open with change.** The first sentence names an unexpected moment. Not "Founded in 2014." Not "Our story begins…" The opener is the ignition point — the day something broke for the founder.
   > Bad: *"In 2014, after years in the industry, [Founder] saw an opportunity."*
   > Good: *"The night the third customer in a month asked for a refund, [Founder] sat in the truck and stopped pretending he had it figured out."*

2. **Establish the flaw — show it working.** A scene from the founder's earlier life where the sacred flaw paid off. The hero-maker moment. Reader sees competence, charisma, conviction. They like the founder.

3. **The story event hits.** The thing the old theory of control can't handle. Customer churn. Co-founder leaving. Market shift. A quiet personal moment of recognition.

4. **Transformation.** The founder tries another way. It doesn't work cleanly. Acts II and III compressed into a paragraph or two. Pain of change (Act IV) gets a sentence. **Do not skip Act IV** — without it the arc is unearned.

5. **The "God moment" = the company today.** Not a sales pitch. A description of the rebuilt theory of control, expressed as how the business now operates. The company is the answer to the founder's flaw.

### Length

500-1,200 words. Long enough for arc. Short enough to read straight through.

### What to omit

- Year-by-year milestones (move to a separate Timeline page)
- Awards (move to footer or trust section)
- Mission statements ("Our mission is to…")
- Lists of values
- Photos of buildings

### What to include

- One named moment of change
- The flaw, named in the founder's voice
- One concrete origin scene (often a single line is enough)
- The story event, specific
- A clear transformation
- A present-day "God moment"

## Case Study

The **customer** is the hero. The product is the agent that lets the customer rebuild their theory of control.

### Structure

1. **Open with the customer's normal life — and the small first crack.** Sensory detail. Specific. Named.
   > *"The kitchen smelled like cinnamon. Lisa was hosting fifteen people in four hours when she saw the first cockroach."*

2. **Customer's sacred flaw (named, not stated).** Show it through behaviour, not adjectives. *"A good host has a perfect house"* is implied by her actions, not announced.

3. **Story event escalates.** The first attempt to handle it the old way fails. The flaw is exposed.

4. **The product enters as agent of change.** Not as hero. Not as savior. As the thing that gives the customer a working alternative to their broken theory of control.

5. **Transformation.** What the customer can now do that they couldn't before. Specific. Sensory.

6. **God moment.** A single image of the rebuilt life. Not "she's happy now" — show her hosting the next dinner party without checking the corners.

### Length

400-800 words. A case study is a short story, not a brochure.

### Anti-patterns

- Starting with the brand
- Treating the product as the hero
- Listing features
- Skipping the customer's internal struggle
- Ending with statistics instead of a scene

## Brand Origin Story

Same shape as the founder narrative, but compressed and stylized. Usually 200-400 words. Lives on About page, in pitch decks, in investor materials.

### One-paragraph compression

> *[Founder] spent [N years] doing [thing]. [Sacred flaw] worked — until [story event]. [Transformation in a phrase]. [Company name] is what that change became.*

Use when full narrative is too long. Lift from the longer About page.

## Blog Hook / Long-form Opener

The first 150 words of a long-form piece. Job: get the reader to keep reading.

### Rules

1. **First sentence = change.** Or threatened change. Or a curiosity gap.
   > Bad: *"Pest control is an important part of home maintenance."*
   > Good: *"The first time we sprayed for termites and they came back six weeks later, I realized everything I'd been taught about the chemistry was wrong."*

2. **Plant the dramatic question in the first paragraph.** Not the answer.

3. **Pose a puzzle the reader thinks they have some idea about but isn't sure.** That's the shape of curiosity.

4. **Use specific, sensory detail.** Three concrete qualities per object that matters.

5. **No throat-clearing.** No "In today's world…" No "It's no secret that…" No definitions of obvious terms.

## Long-form Sales Page

Reader-as-hero. The page walks them through their own flaw → ignition → transformation arc, framed as their decision.

### Structure

1. **Open with their change.** A symptom they recognize. Specific.

2. **Name their sacred flaw — gently.** Not as accusation. As recognition.
   > *"You've been telling yourself the problem is X. Most people in your position do. Here's what's actually happening."*

3. **Story event.** What is making the flaw untenable *right now*. Often the macro market shift, the new constraint, the recent loss.

4. **Alternative theory of control.** Introduce the product as the lever for a new model.

5. **Transformation case (one or two scenes).** A real customer who walked this arc. Specific. Sensory.

6. **Pain of change (Act IV).** Acknowledge that change is hard. Name the most likely objection. Don't pretend the new path is frictionless.

7. **God moment.** The vision of the reader's life after. Concrete. Visual.

8. **CTA.** From StoryBrand. Specific direct CTA + transitional CTA. (This is where the two skills layer.)

### Length

1,500-4,000 words. Test by reading aloud — every paragraph must either advance the arc or close an objection.

## How long should each surface take?

Rough effort budget:

| Surface | Worksheet | Drafting | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Founder narrative / About | Full (Steps 1-8) | 1-2 hours | half-day |
| Case study | Compressed (Steps 1, 6, 7, 8) | 30-60 min | 1-2 hours |
| Brand origin (short) | Lift from founder narrative | 15 min | 30 min |
| Blog hook | Steps 1, 7 only | 15 min | 30 min |
| Long-form sales | Full | 2-4 hours | day |

The worksheet front-loads the thinking. Drafting is faster after it's filled in.
