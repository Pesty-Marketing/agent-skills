# Voice and Grammar Rules

## Brand Voice: How to Develop "Youness"

Voice is not grammar. It's the personality of your business expressed through word choice, cadence, and attitude. Done right, it attracts the like-minded and repels those who aren't a good fit.

Test: Mask your logo. Read your copy aloud. Do you sound like yourself — or like every competitor in your space?

### Step 1: Define four voice dimensions (Nielsen Norman Group framework)

Rate where you fall on each scale:

1. **Funny ←→ Serious**: Do you attempt humor? Or communicate straight-faced?
2. **Formal ←→ Casual**: Arm's-length expert? Or trusted friend with hands-on advice?
3. **Respectful ←→ Irreverent**: Courteous and professional? Or saucy and snarky?
4. **Enthusiastic ←→ Matter-of-fact**: High energy? Or calm and direct?

Pick four adjectives that capture where you land. Be specific.

> Bad choices: "friendly," "reliable," "honest" — table stakes for every business, not differentiating.
> Bad choices: "cutting-edge," "revolutionary," "proactive" — meaningless buzzwords.
> Good choices: "plain-spoken," "practical," "a little irreverent," "direct" — specific enough to make choices against.

### Step 2: Translate each word into a style guide

For each adjective, write 2-3 sentences: "How does this show up? What does it sound like? What does it not sound like?"

Example (MarketingProfs = Enjoyable, Accessible, Affiliative, Smart):
> Enjoyable: "We aren't funny, but we aren't serious either. We want people to smile when they read our words."

### Step 3: Document it

One-page brand voice guide: 4 adjectives + style notes + a do/don't example for each.

### Step 4: Apply it to nonobvious places

Most companies apply voice to marketing copy. Apply it everywhere:
- Error messages
- Confirmation emails
- Customer service scripts
- 404 pages
- Invoice subject lines

### Note on Tone vs. Voice

Voice doesn't change. Tone does — based on context.

Your voice might be "warm and a little cheeky." But if a customer is angry about a service failure, cheekiness is a major turnoff. In those moments, warm stays. Cheeky goes.

## Contractions Are Required

Use don't, shouldn't, we'll, you're, it's. Avoiding contractions makes copy sound like a legal document or a robot.

> Stiff: "We do not recommend applying this product in cold weather."
> Human: "Don't apply this in cold weather."

## Banned Words

### Frankenwords (over-suffixed, bloated)

Words bulked up with -ment, -tion, -ization, -ance that have a simpler version.

| Frankenword | Use instead |
|---|---|
| prioritization | priority |
| revolutionized | changed |
| commercialization | sales |
| incentivization | encouragement |
| differentiation | difference |
| application (as verb) | applied |

Pattern: "Rudolph made an application for the job." → "Rudolph applied for the job."

### Weblish (tech-robot words applied to humans)

Words that have no business being applied to people:

- bandwidth ("I don't have the bandwidth") → time
- ping ("Let me ping you") → reach out / get back to you
- radar screen → on my radar / I'm watching
- execute on → do / deliver
- leverage → use
- learnings → lessons / what we found
- top resource → (just say what you mean)

Test: "Would you whisper this word to your sweetheart in the dark?" If no, don't use it in marketing.

### Buzzword ban (delete on sight)

synergy, cutting-edge, best-of-breed, growth-hacked, impactful, paradigm-shift, leverage, innovative, revolutionary, proactive, robust, seamless, world-class, best-in-class, game-changing, unlock, transform

### Nouns masquerading as verbs

workshopping, journaling, bucketize, incentivize, leverage (as verb), solutioning

## Grammar Defaults

### Active voice over passive

> Passive: "The mistake was made by our team."
> Active: "We messed up."

Passive voice distances the speaker from the action. Use active unless you have a specific reason not to.

### Ditch weakling verbs

Avoid: is, am, are, was, were, be, being, been (as main verbs). Replace with strong action verbs.

> Weak: "Our team is focused on customer success."
> Strong: "Our team obsesses over customer success."

### Adverbs are usually clutter

If a verb is strong enough, it doesn't need an adverb. Most -ly words are filler.

> Weak: "He ran quickly."
> Strong: "He sprinted."

> Weak: "We're very committed to quality."
> Strong: "We're committed to quality." (Or better: name what the commitment looks like.)

### Default to present tense

Present tense is more immediate, direct, and clear.

> Past perfect: "Zero-party data has changed everything."
> Present: "Zero-party data changes everything."

> Wordy past: "We have noticed that four-day workweeks have become standard."
> Present: "Four-day workweeks are now standard."

### Humor comes on the rewrite

Don't try to be funny in the first draft. Add humor in the Shine phase — Draft 4 or later. Humor should be subtle and never at the reader's expense.

---

## Avoid Hot Dog Writing

Hot dog writing = when you're writing to show off rather than to serve the reader. Signs:
- Long words where short ones work
- Complex sentences where simple ones would do
- References to your own expertise rather than the reader's problem
- Conclusions that tell the reader how smart the piece was

Rule: If removing a sentence doesn't hurt the reader's understanding, cut it.
