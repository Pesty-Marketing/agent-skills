# Sacred Flaw Worksheet — 8 Steps

Will Storr's framework from the appendix of *The Science of Storytelling*. Run in order. The worksheet builds the character before any sentence of the actual narrative is drafted.

## 0. Embrace the rewind

Pick where you're starting from:

- **A milieu** (a setting) — zoom in until you find a specific person with a specific flaw.
- **A what-if** — treat it as a story event; ask what flawed person would be maximally broken by it.
- **An argument** — ask whose flaw the argument would most expose. (e.g. *war makes monsters of men* → the narcissistic, rebellious self → T.E. Lawrence)
- **A character** — go straight to Step 1.

---

## 1. SACRED FLAW

A flaw in the character's **theory of control** that they have made *sacred* — built identity around. Not a quirk. Not a vice. A belief about how the world must be run for them to feel safe and worthy.

> Haidt: *"Follow the sacredness. Find out what people believe to be sacred, and when you look around there you will find rampant irrationality."*

Use these sentence stems with the character (or the founder, or the customer). Answer them in the character's voice:

- *The thing people most admire about me is…*
- *I'm only safe when I…*
- *The most important thing of all in life is…*
- *The secret of happiness is…*
- *The best thing about me is…*
- *The most terrible thing about other people is…*
- *The big thing I understand about the world that nobody else seems to get is…*
- *The best advice anyone ever gave me was…*

**Test:** does the answer immediately suggest a *suite of behaviours*?

> Good: *"The best thing about me is I'm always the only adult in the room"* → patronising, stern, hubristic, doesn't listen, takes charge in moments of chaos, distances from peers.
> Good: *"I'm only safe when I enthrall others with tall stories"* → liar, braggart, attention-seeker, performer, exhausting to date.

> Storr: *"You'll know when you have it because you'll feel your character lurch alive in your imagination."*

If you don't feel that, the flaw isn't sharp enough. Try another stem.

---

## 2. ORIGIN DAMAGE (a specific scene)

Write a full scene from before age 21 — characters, setting, dialogue — that ends with a precise lesson the child takes from it.

Format:

> *"If I don't believe/behave like THIS, then THAT might happen."*

Two components are mandatory:
- It tells them **who they must be** to get what they want.
- It tells them **how to avoid** what they fear.

> Lawrence (worked example): *"If I act with vain rebellion, I won't be made to feel invisible by people I admire."*

**Avoid clichés** ("her father beat her"). Be specific. You can excise this scene from the final draft — but YOU must know it. Shakespeare's breakthrough was knowing it and *radically excising* it (Hamlet, Iago, Lear).

For business applications: the origin damage usually maps to early career, family pressure, an early failure, or an early validation that became identity-defining.

---

## 3. PERSONALITY (Big Five lens)

Run the character + flaw through the Big Five and decide where they sit on each (low / mid / high):

- **Extraversion** — assertive, dominant, sociable vs reserved, withdrawn
- **Neuroticism** — anxious, reactive, easily destabilised vs calm, steady
- **Openness** — curious, novelty-seeking vs conventional, traditional
- **Agreeableness** — warm, cooperative vs cold, competitive
- **Conscientiousness** — disciplined, dutiful vs impulsive, careless

The Big Five are dials, not switches. The flaw expresses itself differently depending on the dial settings. A "must be the only adult" flaw in a high-neuroticism / low-agreeableness person reads as controlling and bitter; the same flaw in a high-extraversion / high-conscientiousness person reads as charismatic-but-overbearing.

---

## 4. HERO-MAKER MOMENT (a second scene)

Write a scene, before age 21, where the character is *actively challenged* and uses the flawed belief — and it **WORKS**. Their superpower. They defend it (out loud, to an antagonist, or as inner monologue), wielding any of:

- Moral virtue ("I was the only one telling the truth")
- David-vs-Goliath framing ("I was outnumbered and won anyway")
- "I deserve more status" ("They underestimated me")
- "I'm selfless, they're selfish"

> Storr's exemplar: the *"You can't handle the truth"* speech from *A Few Good Men*.

From this scene on, the flaw is **sacred**. They will defend it to the death because it has *proven itself*.

For business stories: this is the early-career win, the first big client, the moment the founder's contrarian instinct paid off. It cements the theory of control.

---

## 5. POINT OF VIEW (the Baldwin exercise)

Storr's drill: rewrite a richly-described passage (he uses Baldwin's Harlem jazz-club scene) from your character's POV.

For business use, swap in a vivid setting your character would actually walk into — a competitor's office, an industry conference floor, a customer's kitchen — and write 200-300 words of what they notice, in their voice.

Ask:
- What do they fix on?
- What hero-maker narrative runs in their head?
- What do they say to themselves to feel better when reality threatens the flaw?

This is the "voice calibration." If you can write 300 words in this voice without stalling, you know the character. If not, return to Step 1.

---

## 6. CHARACTERISTIC WORLD

Answer every one of these specifically — no abstractions:

- How has the flaw led to **material/career gains**?
- How does it give them a felt sense of **superior status**?
- What small moments of **joy** does it bring?
- How has it brought **closeness** with friends/colleagues/lovers?
- What **life goals** has it generated? What external achievement do they believe will complete them?
- What will they **risk losing** (materially / socially) if they act against it?
- On the subconscious level, what do they **ultimately dread** will happen if they act against the flaw?

The last two are the load-bearing answers. They are the engine of every choice the character makes in the plot.

---

## 7. STORY EVENT

The real-world happening that will overwhelm and ultimately break the sacred flaw. Pick one (Storr's list):

- An opportunity
- A plot or conspiracy
- A journey or quest
- An investigation
- A misunderstanding by a powerful figure
- A revelation
- A promotion or demotion
- An enemy or unwelcome figure from the past
- An accusation
- An onerous task
- A discovery
- A rescue
- A reckoning
- A dare or challenge
- An injustice
- An escape
- An attack
- A temptation
- A betrayal

**Critical:** the story event must strike at the *specific* flaw. A generic shock won't do. The event must be the one thing this character's theory of control can't handle.

For business stories: the story event is usually the moment the old way of operating stopped working — a client loss, a regulatory shift, a product failure, a market collapse, a partner's betrayal, a quiet personal revelation.

---

## 8. PLOT (the 5-act spine)

Storr's framing of the canonical structure. Each act is a phase of the character's relationship to their flaw:

- **Act I — *This is me, and it's not working.***
  Establish flaw, world, goal, secret wound. Ignition point hits. Character reacts characteristically. It fails.

- **Act II — *Is there another way?***
  Character experiments with a new strategy. A mentor, an ally, a contrarian voice appears. A small (illusory) win. They commit to the fight.

- **Act III — *There is. I have transformed.***
  Mid-story: full, dramatic embrace of the new theory of control. Feels irreversible. **Plot strikes back with unprecedented force.** Every fear the old flaw protected against now happens.

- **Act IV — *Can I handle the pain of change?***
  Dark night. Loved ones lost. Hints of origin damage surface. Character may retreat to old self.

- **Act V — *Who am I going to be?***
  Final battle. The **"God moment"** — total control over both external world and internal self. Dramatic question answered conclusively.

  (Tragic version: doubles down on flaw, leading to humiliation / ostracization / death.)

---

## Worked example: *The Godfather*

- **Sacred flaw:** *"I'm an honest, upstanding family man, not a gangster."*
- **Dramatic question:** *Am I an honest, upstanding family man? Or a gangster?*
- **Origin damage:** Favorite son; father dreamed of him as senator.
- **Hero-maker moment:** War record, Ivy League education, the all-American girlfriend.
- **Story event:** The family comes under attack.
- **Plot:** Acts I-V end with Kay asking *"Is it true?"* — Michael lies. Hand-kiss. Fade to black. He has answered the dramatic question — and become the thing he most denied being.

---

## What this worksheet is FOR (and not for)

**Use when** the surface is read linearly and character matters: founder story, About page, case study, brand origin, long-form sales, blog hook.

**Don't use** for scan-first marketing surfaces (homepage, ad headline, one-liner). Those need StoryBrand's BrandScript instead — different tool, different brain mode.

**Pair with** the BrandScript when the same project needs both a marketing surface AND a story-driven About/founder page. Build the BrandScript first (it's faster), then the Sacred Flaw worksheet for the narrative pieces.
